Born A Crime

Born a Crime by Trevor Noah is an autobiographical book that recounts the comedian and television
personality's experiences growing up as a mixed-race child in apartheid South Africa. The memoir
provides insights into Noah's life, as well as the culture, systems, and history of South Africa
during this period.
Noah was born in 1984 to a single mother, Patricia, who defied the racist laws of apartheid by having
a child with a white Swiss immigrant, Robert. Despite the danger and social stigma associated with their
relationship, Robert never supported segregation and did what he could to undermine apartheid. Noah often
felt caught between racial groups, not light enough to be fully accepted into white culture and not dark
enough to be fully accepted into black culture.
Apartheid ended in 1994, but Noah continued to face struggles as a mixed-race child in South Africa.
He had to navigate different social settings and determine which groups would accept him. Patricia
encouraged her son to read and think independently, instilling in him a love for education and multiple
languages (Zulu, Tsonga, English, and Afrikaans). This skill allowed him to move among different racial and
cultural groups at school.
Noah's experiences included dealing with law enforcement his mother received no help from the local police
when she tried to file charges against her abusive husband and being arrested himself for various offences.
Despite these challenges, Noah recognized his privilege compared to many of his peers who faced greater hardships.
The relationships with the men in Noah's life taught him little about interacting with women. His father was a distant
figure, while his stepfather Abel became violent towards Patricia after they married.
