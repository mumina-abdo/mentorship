Born A Crime

Noah was born in 1984 to a single mother, Patricia, who defied the racistlaws of apartheid by having a child with a white Swiss immigrant, Robert. Despite the danger and social stigma associated with their relationship, Robert never supported segregation and did what he could to undermine apartheid.

Noah often felt caught between racial groups, not light enough to be fully acceptedinto white culture and not dark enough to be fully accepted into black culture. Apartheid ended in 1994, but Noah continued to face struggles as a mixed-race child in South Africa.

He had to navigate different social settings and determine which groups would accept him. Patricia encouraged her son to read and think independently, instilling in him a love for education and multiple languages (Zulu, Tsonga, English, and Afrikaans)


In "Born a Crime," Noah talks about the apartheid system in South Africa, which saw racial segregation enforced by the government. Similar inter-ethnic disputes and tensions have occurred in Marsabit County, resulting in discrimination and racial segregation among people of all races. The challenges encountered by people from diverse ethnic backgrounds in Marsabit County bear similarities to the racial differences portrayed in Noah's narrative.
